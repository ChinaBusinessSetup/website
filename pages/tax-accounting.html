Outsource Your China Accounting, Bookkeeping, and Tax Filing — 2026 Guide for Foreign Companies

Managing accounting in China is not simply a back-office task. For foreign-invested enterprises, it is a core compliance function that directly affects tax reporting, cash flow, payroll, customs, foreign exchange, banking, annual inspections, and the company’s ability to operate smoothly.

If your business has registered a Wholly Foreign-Owned Enterprise (WFOE), Joint Venture, Representative Office, or other legal entity in China, you are required to maintain accounting records under Chinese rules, file taxes on time, issue compliant invoices, and meet annual reporting obligations.

Many foreign founders assume that China accounting can be handled the same way as in their home country. In practice, China has its own accounting standards, tax filing cadence, invoice system, bookkeeping requirements, and government reporting platforms. This is why many international companies choose to outsource their China accounting, bookkeeping, and tax filing to a local professional provider.

This 2026 guide explains how China accounting works, what monthly and annual compliance tasks are required, when outsourcing makes sense, and how to choose the right accounting partner for your business.

Why China Accounting Is Different from Other Markets

China’s accounting and tax system is highly structured, document-driven, and closely connected to government digital platforms. A company must not only prepare internal management accounts but also maintain statutory books that comply with Chinese Accounting Standards.

For foreign companies, this usually means maintaining a local accounting system in Chinese, using China’s chart of accounts, recording valid supporting documents, and filing taxes with the local tax bureau according to monthly, quarterly, and annual deadlines.

China accounting is different because it involves:

  • Statutory bookkeeping under Chinese Accounting Standards
  • Tax filings through local tax bureau systems
  • Fapiao management and invoice verification
  • VAT input and output reconciliation
  • Payroll and Individual Income Tax withholding
  • Social insurance and housing fund contributions
  • Annual corporate income tax reconciliation
  • Audit-ready documentation
  • Cross-border payment support
  • Foreign exchange compliance

A global finance team may prepare consolidated reports under IFRS or US GAAP, but the China entity still needs its own compliant local books. These local books are the basis for tax filings, statutory reporting, bank reviews, audits, and regulatory inspections.

What Is Included in China Accounting, Bookkeeping, and Tax Filing?

Outsourced accounting services in China usually cover a combination of monthly bookkeeping, tax filing, payroll support, financial reporting, and annual compliance.

Monthly Bookkeeping

Monthly bookkeeping involves recording all financial transactions of the China entity.

Typical tasks include:

  • Recording sales revenue
  • Recording supplier payments
  • Booking staff reimbursements
  • Reconciling bank transactions
  • Checking expense documentation
  • Reviewing contracts and invoices
  • Recording fixed assets
  • Maintaining statutory ledgers
  • Preparing monthly financial statements

Accurate bookkeeping is essential because tax filings in China rely heavily on properly recorded transactions and valid supporting documents.

VAT Filing

Value Added Tax is one of the most important taxes for companies operating in China. VAT applies to the sale of goods, provision of services, import of goods, and many cross-border transactions.

China’s VAT system uses different rates depending on the business activity. Common rates include:

  • 13% for many goods and certain services
  • 9% for specific goods and sectors
  • 6% for many modern services
  • 0% for certain eligible export transactions

From 2026, China’s VAT Law provides a more formal statutory framework for VAT administration. Companies should review their VAT treatment, invoice processes, input VAT credit eligibility, and cross-border transaction documentation to ensure compliance.

Corporate Income Tax Filing

Corporate Income Tax applies to company profits. The standard CIT rate in China is generally 25%, although certain qualified companies may be eligible for preferential rates, such as high-tech enterprise incentives or encouraged industry policies.

China companies normally need to make periodic CIT prepayments and complete annual CIT reconciliation after the year-end. This process compares the company’s accounting profit with taxable income after tax adjustments.

Common CIT adjustments include:

  • Non-deductible expenses
  • Entertainment expense limits
  • Employee welfare expense limits
  • Depreciation adjustments
  • Related-party transaction adjustments
  • Unqualified invoices
  • Accrual timing differences

Poor bookkeeping during the year often creates problems during annual CIT reconciliation.

Individual Income Tax and Payroll Compliance

Employers in China are responsible for withholding and filing Individual Income Tax for employees.

Payroll compliance may include:

  • Monthly salary calculation
  • IIT withholding
  • Social insurance contributions
  • Housing fund contributions
  • Expatriate employee tax registration
  • Annual IIT reconciliation support
  • Payroll reports and payslips

Foreign companies must pay close attention to employee classification, salary structure, benefits, allowances, and cross-border compensation arrangements.

Fapiao Management

Fapiao is China’s official invoice system. It is more than a commercial invoice. It is a tax control document used to support revenue recognition, VAT output, expense deductions, and input VAT credits.

Fapiao management includes:

  • Issuing sales fapiao
  • Verifying supplier fapiao
  • Matching invoices with contracts and payments
  • Tracking input VAT credits
  • Avoiding invalid or non-deductible invoices
  • Managing electronic fapiao records

For many foreign companies, fapiao is one of the most unfamiliar aspects of China accounting.

Why Foreign Companies Outsource China Accounting

Outsourcing China accounting, bookkeeping, and tax filing is common because local compliance requires specialized knowledge, Chinese-language filings, and constant attention to deadlines.

Reduce Compliance Risk

Late filings, incorrect tax calculations, missing fapiao, and unsupported deductions can lead to penalties, tax adjustments, and increased scrutiny from authorities.

An experienced accounting provider helps reduce these risks by maintaining proper books, filing taxes on time, and identifying issues before they become costly.

Save Internal Resources

Hiring a full internal finance team in China can be expensive, especially for early-stage companies, consulting firms, trading businesses, and startups.

Outsourcing allows companies to access local accounting expertise without building a full in-house department from day one.

Improve Communication with Authorities

Local accountants understand tax bureau requirements, filing systems, documentation standards, and common questions raised by government officials.

This is especially useful when handling tax registration, VAT matters, annual filings, or historical compliance issues.

Support Group Reporting

A good accounting provider can prepare local statutory books while also supporting overseas reporting requirements.

This may include:

  • Monthly management accounts
  • Balance sheet and profit and loss reports
  • Cash flow reports
  • Expense breakdowns
  • Intercompany transaction reports
  • IFRS or US GAAP reporting packages

Prepare for Audit and Annual Compliance

China entities must maintain proper accounting records throughout the year. Waiting until year-end to organize documents often creates delays, errors, and higher costs.

Outsourced bookkeeping helps keep the company audit-ready.

Monthly China Accounting Workflow

A structured monthly workflow helps foreign companies stay compliant and avoid last-minute filing problems.

Step 1: Collect Source Documents

The company provides all monthly documents to the accounting team, including:

  • Bank statements
  • Sales contracts
  • Purchase contracts
  • Fapiao
  • Expense receipts
  • Payroll data
  • Reimbursement forms
  • Import/export documents
  • Loan or capital injection records

Documents should be complete, consistent, and properly matched.

Step 2: Review Documentation

The accountant checks whether transactions are supported by valid documents.

This review may identify:

  • Missing fapiao
  • Incorrect invoice titles
  • Non-deductible expenses
  • Mismatched contracts and payments
  • VAT credit issues
  • Unclear employee reimbursements

Early review helps avoid tax problems later.

Step 3: Record Transactions

Transactions are recorded in the China statutory accounting system according to Chinese Accounting Standards.

This includes:

  • Revenue recognition
  • Expense booking
  • VAT treatment
  • Payroll accruals
  • Asset capitalization
  • Bank reconciliation
  • Intercompany balances

Step 4: Prepare Tax Calculations

The accountant calculates tax liabilities, including VAT, CIT prepayments, surcharges, IIT, and other applicable taxes.

Step 5: Submit Tax Filings

Tax filings are submitted through the relevant local tax bureau system before the deadline.

Step 6: Provide Monthly Reports

The company receives financial reports for management review.

Typical reports include:

  • Balance sheet
  • Profit and loss statement
  • Tax filing summary
  • Bank reconciliation
  • Accounts receivable and payable summary
  • Payroll summary

Annual Compliance Requirements in China

Monthly bookkeeping is only part of the compliance cycle. China companies also need to complete annual reporting and tax reconciliation.

Annual Corporate Income Tax Reconciliation

Companies must reconcile annual taxable income and submit the annual CIT filing after the fiscal year ends.

This process requires a detailed review of:

  • Revenue
  • Costs
  • Expenses
  • Tax adjustments
  • Related-party transactions
  • Loss carryforwards
  • Preferential tax treatment
  • Deductibility of expenses

For most companies, annual CIT reconciliation is one of the most important tax compliance tasks of the year.

Annual Audit

Many foreign-invested enterprises are expected to prepare audited financial statements for statutory, tax, shareholder, or group reporting purposes.

An audit reviews whether the company’s financial statements fairly reflect its financial position and whether accounting records comply with applicable standards.

Annual Business Reporting

Companies must also complete annual reporting with relevant government authorities. Information may include:

  • Shareholder details
  • Registered capital status
  • Business operations
  • Financial information
  • Social insurance information
  • Contact details

Failure to complete annual reporting can affect the company’s credit record.

Key Tax Types for Foreign-Invested Companies in China

Value Added Tax

VAT applies to most commercial activities in China. Companies must correctly determine VAT rates, issue fapiao, collect output VAT, and claim eligible input VAT credits.

Common VAT risks include:

  • Incorrect VAT rate application
  • Invalid input VAT claims
  • Missing fapiao
  • Incorrect export VAT treatment
  • Poor documentation for cross-border services

Corporate Income Tax

CIT is levied on taxable profits. The standard rate is generally 25%, with possible preferential rates for qualifying businesses.

Common CIT risks include:

  • Unsupported deductions
  • Excessive related-party service fees
  • Unreasonable management charges
  • Incorrect depreciation treatment
  • Failure to make annual tax adjustments

Individual Income Tax

Employers must withhold IIT from salaries and certain benefits.

Common IIT risks include:

  • Incorrect expatriate tax treatment
  • Unreported allowances
  • Improper bonus calculation
  • Late payroll filing
  • Incorrect social insurance base

Stamp Duty

Stamp duty may apply to certain contracts, business documents, and transactions.

Surcharges and Local Levies

VAT taxpayers may also be subject to urban maintenance and construction tax, education surcharge, and local education surcharge.

Special Considerations for Foreign Companies

Cross-Border Payments

Payments from a China company to overseas related parties often require careful review.

Examples include:

  • Service fees
  • Royalties
  • Interest
  • Dividends
  • Cost reimbursements
  • Management fees

These payments may involve withholding tax, VAT, transfer pricing documentation, bank review, and foreign exchange procedures.

Transfer Pricing

Companies with related-party transactions must ensure that pricing is commercially reasonable and properly documented.

Related-party transactions may include:

  • Intercompany services
  • Sale of goods
  • Licensing of intellectual property
  • Loans
  • Cost sharing arrangements

China tax authorities may review whether transactions have business substance and whether pricing follows arm’s length principles.

Foreign Exchange

China maintains foreign exchange controls. Certain inbound and outbound payments require documentation and bank review.

Accounting records should support the purpose, amount, and nature of cross-border payments.

Import and Export Businesses

Trading and manufacturing companies must coordinate accounting with customs, VAT, and logistics documentation.

Important documents include:

  • Customs declarations
  • Import VAT records
  • Export documents
  • Freight invoices
  • Supplier contracts
  • Customer contracts
  • Foreign currency settlement records

Common Accounting Mistakes in China

Treating Overseas Invoices as Automatically Deductible

Not every overseas invoice is deductible for China tax purposes. Companies need proper contracts, payment records, tax treatment analysis, and supporting documents.

Ignoring Fapiao Requirements

Expenses without valid fapiao may not be deductible for CIT or creditable for VAT.

Filing Taxes Without Reviewing the Books

Tax filing should be based on accurate bookkeeping. Filing numbers without reconciling bank accounts, invoices, and contracts increases risk.

Underestimating Payroll Compliance

Payroll mistakes can create IIT, social insurance, labor law, and employee relations problems.

Waiting Until Year-End

China compliance is a monthly process. Delaying bookkeeping until the end of the year often leads to missing documents, inaccurate filings, and audit delays.

Should You Hire In-House or Outsource?

For many foreign companies, outsourcing is the most practical option in the early stages.

Outsourcing Is Suitable If:

  • You have a small or medium-sized China entity
  • You need local compliance support
  • You do not have a full finance team in China
  • You want bilingual reporting
  • You need predictable monthly costs
  • You want external review of tax risk

In-House Finance May Be Suitable If:

  • You have high transaction volume
  • You operate multiple entities
  • You have complex manufacturing operations
  • You need daily finance control
  • You have extensive internal approval procedures

Many companies use a hybrid model: an internal finance coordinator handles daily operations, while an external provider manages statutory accounting, tax filing, and annual compliance.

FAQ: China Accounting, Bookkeeping, and Tax Filing

Do foreign companies need local accounting books in China?

Yes. A China-registered company must maintain statutory accounting records that comply with Chinese accounting requirements. Overseas management accounts alone are not sufficient.

How often do companies file taxes in China?

Most companies file taxes monthly or quarterly depending on tax type, taxpayer status, and local requirements. VAT and IIT are commonly handled on a monthly basis, while CIT may involve periodic prepayments and annual reconciliation.

What is fapiao?

Fapiao is China’s official tax invoice. It is essential for VAT compliance, expense deductions, revenue documentation, and tax reporting.

Can a foreign parent company manage China accounting from overseas?

The parent company can supervise reporting, but local statutory bookkeeping and tax filing must comply with China rules and usually require local system access and Chinese-language documentation.

What happens if tax filings are late?

Late filings may result in penalties, late payment interest, tax bureau inquiries, and potential restrictions on company operations.

Is outsourcing China accounting cheaper than hiring staff?

For small and medium-sized foreign-invested enterprises, outsourcing is often more cost-effective than hiring a full in-house finance team.

Do Representative Offices need accounting and tax filing?

Yes. Representative Offices still have accounting, tax, payroll, and annual compliance obligations, even though they cannot conduct direct revenue-generating business.

Do companies need an annual audit in China?

Many foreign-invested companies prepare annual audit reports for statutory, tax, shareholder, or group reporting purposes. Requirements may vary depending on the company type, location, and business needs.

Can accounting records be kept in English?

Management reports can be prepared in English, but statutory accounting records and tax filings in China generally need to follow Chinese requirements.

When should a new company start bookkeeping?

Bookkeeping should start as soon as the company is established and begins incurring expenses, opening bank accounts, hiring employees, or issuing invoices.
