Register and Set Up a Company in China (2026 Guide for Foreign Founders)

China remains one of the world’s largest consumer markets, manufacturing hubs, and innovation ecosystems. Despite global economic shifts, foreign investors continue to establish businesses in China to access its domestic market, strengthen supply chains, and expand across Asia.

For entrepreneurs and international companies, understanding how to register and set up a company in China is the first step toward building a successful market presence.

The process has become significantly more streamlined over the past decade. Digital government platforms, Free Trade Zones (FTZs), and ongoing foreign investment reforms have reduced administrative barriers and improved transparency for overseas investors. At the same time, new compliance requirements—including the updated Company Law and registered capital rules—mean that proper planning is more important than ever.

This guide explains everything foreign founders need to know in 2026, including company structures, registration procedures, timelines, costs, tax considerations, and common mistakes to avoid.

Can Foreigners Register a Company in China?

Yes.

Foreign individuals and overseas companies can legally establish businesses in China, provided their proposed activities are permitted under China’s Foreign Investment framework.

Since the implementation of the Foreign Investment Law, foreign investors generally receive national treatment in sectors that are not restricted under the Foreign Investment Negative List. As a result, foreign entrepreneurs can own 100% of a company in many industries without requiring a Chinese partner.

Today, foreign-owned companies operate across a wide range of sectors, including:

  • International trade
  • Consulting services
  • Software development
  • Manufacturing
  • E-commerce
  • Supply chain management
  • Technology services
  • Professional services

Why Register a Company in China?

Many businesses initially enter China through distributors or sourcing agents. However, establishing a legal entity provides significantly greater control and flexibility.

Benefits include:

Direct Access to the Chinese Market

A locally registered company can:

  • Issue official Chinese invoices (Fapiao)
  • Sign contracts directly with Chinese customers
  • Hire employees locally
  • Open corporate bank accounts
  • Receive RMB payments

Full Operational Control

Unlike representative offices, registered companies can conduct revenue-generating activities and operate independently.

This is especially important for:

  • Service providers
  • Trading businesses
  • Manufacturers
  • Technology companies

Stronger Brand Presence

Chinese customers often prefer dealing with locally registered companies rather than overseas entities.

A China company can improve:

  • Customer trust
  • Supplier relationships
  • Government interactions
  • Market credibility

Choosing the Right Business Structure

Before starting the registration process, foreign investors must choose the appropriate legal entity.

Wholly Foreign-Owned Enterprise (WFOE)

The WFOE remains the most popular structure for foreign investors.

A WFOE is a limited liability company that is 100% owned by foreign individuals or overseas companies. It provides complete operational control and legal independence.

Advantages

  • 100% foreign ownership
  • Direct hiring rights
  • Ability to generate revenue
  • Strong intellectual property protection
  • Independent decision-making

Best For

  • Consulting firms
  • Trading companies
  • Technology startups
  • Manufacturers
  • E-commerce businesses

Joint Venture (JV)

A Joint Venture combines foreign investment with a Chinese partner.

Although less common than in the past, JVs remain useful when:

  • Local licenses are required
  • Industry expertise is needed
  • Distribution networks are critical

Advantages

  • Access to local market knowledge
  • Existing business relationships
  • Shared operational resources

Considerations

Partner selection is crucial. Poor governance structures can lead to conflicts and operational challenges.

Representative Office (RO)

A Representative Office allows overseas companies to establish a presence in China without creating a separate revenue-generating entity.

However, ROs cannot:

  • Invoice customers
  • Receive business income
  • Conduct direct sales

Best For

  • Market research
  • Supplier management
  • Liaison activities

Foreign-Invested Partnership (FIP)

This structure is commonly used for:

  • Investment projects
  • Consulting partnerships
  • Professional service firms

Although flexible, it is less common than WFOEs.

Step-by-Step Process to Register and Set Up a Company in China

Step 1: Determine Business Activities

Your business scope defines what activities the company can legally perform.

Examples include:

  • Import and export
  • Technology consulting
  • Software development
  • Manufacturing
  • Marketing services

Choosing the correct scope at the beginning helps avoid future licensing complications.

Step 2: Select the Registration Location

Location matters.

Popular choices include:

Shanghai

Best for:

  • International trade
  • Financial services
  • Regional headquarters

Shenzhen

Best for:

  • Technology companies
  • Startups
  • Hardware innovation

Guangzhou

Best for:

  • Manufacturing
  • Export businesses

Beijing

Best for:

  • Professional services
  • Technology
  • Government-related industries

Free Trade Zones (FTZs)

FTZs offer:

  • Simplified procedures
  • Customs advantages
  • Industry-specific incentives

For many foreign founders, FTZ registration can accelerate market entry.

Step 3: Company Name Approval

The company name typically follows this format:

City + Trade Name + Industry + Co., Ltd.

Example:

Shanghai Global Tech Consulting Co., Ltd.

Authorities review the proposed name before registration can proceed.

Step 4: Prepare Registration Documents

Required documents generally include:

For Individual Shareholders

  • Passport copy
  • Proof of address
  • Personal information forms

For Corporate Shareholders

  • Certificate of incorporation
  • Company registry documents
  • Board resolution
  • Legal representative appointment

Many overseas documents must be notarized and authenticated before use in China.

Step 5: Submit Registration Application

Applications are submitted through local market regulation authorities.

Key documents include:

  • Articles of Association
  • Shareholder information
  • Registered address documentation
  • Business scope declaration

Upon approval, the company receives its Business License.

Step 6: Obtain Company Seals

China uses official company chops for legal authorization.

Typical seals include:

  • Company seal
  • Financial seal
  • Contract seal
  • Legal representative seal

These are essential for daily operations.

Step 7: Open Corporate Bank Accounts

Most companies require:

RMB Basic Account

Used for:

  • Payroll
  • Tax payments
  • Domestic transactions

Foreign Currency Account

Used for:

  • International transfers
  • Capital injections
  • Cross-border payments

Step 8: Tax Registration

After incorporation, companies must complete tax registration and reporting setup.

Common taxes include:

Corporate Income Tax (CIT)

Standard rate:

25%

Eligible high-tech enterprises and certain encouraged industries may qualify for reduced rates.

Value Added Tax (VAT)

VAT rates vary depending on business activities.

Individual Income Tax (IIT)

Applies to employee salaries and compensation.

Registered Capital Requirements in 2026

One of the most important changes affecting foreign investors is the updated registered capital framework.

Under the revised Company Law, shareholders are generally expected to contribute subscribed capital within the statutory contribution period. This makes capital planning more important during company formation.

There is no universal minimum registered capital requirement for most industries.

However, investors should ensure capital levels are realistic enough to support:

  • Office expenses
  • Payroll
  • Operating costs
  • Regulatory expectations

StageEstimated Time
Name Approval1–3 days

Company Registration2–4 weeks

Typical Timeline

8–12 weeks from start to operational readiness.

How Much Does It Cost to Register a Company in China?

Costs vary depending on:

  • City
  • Industry
  • Company structure
  • Licensing requirements

Typical expenses include:

  • Government filings
  • Legal support
  • Registered address services
  • Translation and notarization
  • Accounting setup
  • Bank account assistance

Most foreign investors should budget for both setup costs and ongoing compliance expenses.

Common Mistakes Foreign Founders Should Avoid

Choosing the Wrong Business Scope

Changing business scope later can require additional approvals.

Underestimating Registered Capital

Insufficient capital can affect banking relationships and operational flexibility.

Ignoring Compliance Obligations

Every company must maintain:

  • Accounting records
  • Tax filings
  • Annual reporting
  • Payroll compliance

Selecting the Wrong Location

The best city depends on:

  • Customers
  • Supply chain
  • Talent availability
  • Industry incentives

FAQ: Register and Set Up a Company in China

Can a foreigner own 100% of a company in China?

Yes. Most industries permit 100% foreign ownership through a WFOE structure.

What is the best company type for foreign investors?

For most businesses, a WFOE offers the greatest flexibility, control, and operational freedom.

Do I need a Chinese partner?

Not usually. Only certain restricted industries may require local participation.

How much registered capital is required?

There is no standard minimum for most industries, but capital should be sufficient to support business operations and meet legal requirements.

How long does company registration take?

Most foreign-invested companies can be established within 8–12 weeks.

Can I register a company remotely?

Yes. Many steps can be completed remotely through professional service providers, although certain banking procedures may require in-person verification depending on the bank and location.

Conclusion

For foreign founders, the opportunity to register and set up a company in China has never been more accessible. Reforms to foreign investment rules, digital registration systems, and Free Trade Zone policies have made market entry more efficient than in previous years.

However, successful company formation requires more than simply obtaining a business license. Investors must carefully choose the right entity structure, location, business scope, and compliance strategy to build a sustainable presence in the Chinese market.

Whether you are launching a trading company, consulting business, manufacturing operation, or technology startup, proper planning during the registration stage can save significant time, costs, and regulatory challenges later.

If you are considering expanding into China in 2026, working with experienced company formation and compliance professionals can help ensure a smooth, compliant, and successful market entry.
